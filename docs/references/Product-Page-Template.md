# 2. Purpose

This document defines the standard product page experience used across SERIAN LAB.

Rather than specifying visual layouts, it defines the decision-making journey every product page should guide users through.

Every product page should follow the same narrative structure regardless of product category.

Consistency of user understanding is more important than visual variation.

---

# 3. Product Page Philosophy

A product page is not a sales page.

It is a decision-support experience.

Its purpose is to reduce uncertainty and help users make confident purchasing decisions.

The page should behave like a knowledgeable product consultant rather than an aggressive salesperson.

Explain before selling.

Educate before converting.

Trust before persuasion.

---

# 4. User Journey

Every product page should guide users through the following journey:

Attention

↓

Understanding

↓

Problem Awareness

↓

Solution

↓

Evidence

↓

Trust

↓

Purchase Decision

The experience should feel natural and uninterrupted.

Users should never feel rushed.

---

# 5. Narrative Principles

Every page tells one product story.

Every section answers exactly one question.

Every scroll reveals one new piece of information.

Each section should naturally lead users toward the next question.

Avoid repeating information across multiple sections.

Avoid presenting unrelated information within the same section.

---

# 6. Standard Narrative Flow

## Section 1 — Attention

Primary Question

"What is this?"

Purpose

Capture attention immediately.

Introduce the product visually.

Communicate the product's primary value within seconds.

---

## Section 2 — Problem

Primary Question

"Why should I care?"

Purpose

Help users recognize an existing pain point.

Describe real-life situations rather than marketing claims.

Users should feel understood before being sold to.

---

## Section 3 — Solution

Primary Question

"How does this solve my problem?"

Purpose

Demonstrate the product's core mechanism.

Explain benefits through practical usage rather than technical specifications.

Focus on clarity.

---

## Section 4 — Evidence

Primary Question

"Does it actually work?"

Purpose

Provide visual proof.

Use demonstrations, comparisons, before-and-after examples, or authentic product usage.

Evidence should replace exaggerated claims whenever possible.

---

## Section 5 — Trust

Primary Question

"Why should I trust this?"

Purpose

Reduce hesitation.

Present authentic customer experiences, reviews, guarantees, FAQs, or transparent policies.

Trust should be earned through information rather than urgency.

---

## Section 6 — Purchase Decision

Primary Question

"Am I ready to buy?"

Purpose

Present pricing, purchasing options, shipping information, and the primary call-to-action.

Users should feel confident that they have enough information to make their own decision.

---

# 7. Information Principles

Information should appear progressively.

Each section should introduce one primary idea.

Avoid information overload.

Avoid repeating identical selling points.

The interface should continuously reduce uncertainty.

---

# 8. Content Principles

Content should answer questions instead of making claims.

Prefer demonstrations over adjectives.

Prefer real scenarios over abstract descriptions.

Prefer explanations over slogans.

Avoid unnecessary marketing language.

Users should leave each section with fewer questions than before.

---

# 9. Trust Principles

Trust should be built gradually throughout the page.

Do not rely on urgency, scarcity, countdown timers, or visual pressure.

Confidence should come from transparency, product understanding, and consistent presentation.

Trust is accumulated rather than created by a single section.

---

# 10. Call-to-Action Principles

The call-to-action should appear only after sufficient understanding has been established.

CTA elements should feel like the natural next step rather than an interruption.

The goal is not to pressure users into purchasing.

The goal is to make purchasing effortless once users have decided.

---

# 11. Non-Goals

A product page should not:

* Maximize information density.
* Overwhelm users with marketing language.
* Depend on visual effects to attract attention.
* Interrupt reading with unnecessary interactions.
* Create artificial urgency.
* Compete with the product for attention.

---

# 12. Success Criteria

A successful product page allows users to answer the following questions without confusion:

* What is this product?
* Why does it exist?
* How does it work?
* Why is it better?
* Why should I trust it?
* What should I do next?

If users understand the product before reaching the purchase section, the page has succeeded.

---

# Design North Star

The product should remain the center of attention.

The interface should quietly support understanding.

Every section should reduce uncertainty.

Every interaction should increase confidence.

The page should help users make informed purchasing decisions with calm confidence.
